  \begin{proof}[\textbf{Solution to 1}]
    Given the integral
    \[%
      \iint_D [(x - 2)(x - 2y)]^{\sfrac{1}{2}} \dA
    ,\]%
    we can use the change of variables $u = x - y$ and $v = x - 2y$. Solving in
    terms of $x$ and $y$, we have
    \begin{alignat*}{3}
      \phantom{\implies}\quad&u &&= x - y \implies x = u + y \\
      \implies\quad&v &&= u + y - 2y \implies y = u - v \\
      \implies\quad&x &&= u + (u - v)
    .\end{alignat*}
    Therefore, we have
    \begin{align*}
      x &= 2u - v \\
      y &= u - v
    .\end{align*}
    The Jacobian is
    \[%
      \lvert J \rvert = \jdv{x,y}{u,v} = \begin{vmatrix}
        2 & -1 \\
        1 & -1
      \end{vmatrix} = \lvert -1 \rvert = 1
    .\]%
    Therefore, the integral becomes
    \[%
      \iint_D [(x - 2)(x - 2y)]^{\sfrac{1}{2}} \dA = \iint_{D'} \sqrt{uv} \duv
    ,\]%
    where $D'$ is the image of $D$ under the transformation. To find the bounds
    for $D'$, say $(0, 0), (2, 0), (2, 1)$, we compute the following
    \begin{align*}
      (0, 0) &\implies
      \begin{cases}
        u = 0 - 0 &\implies u = 0 \\
        v = 0 - 2(0) &\implies v = 0
      \end{cases} \\
             &(0, 0) \mapsto (0, 0) \\
      (2, 0) &\implies
      \begin{cases}
        u = 2 - 0 &\implies u = 2 \\
        v = 2 - 2(0) &\implies v = 2
      \end{cases} \\
             &(2, 0) \mapsto (2, 2) \\
      (2, 1) &\implies
      \begin{cases}
        u = 2 - 1 &\implies u = 1 \\
        v = 2 - 2(1) &\implies v = 0
      \end{cases} \\
             &(2, 1) \mapsto (1, 0)
    .\end{align*}
    Therefore, the new bounds for $D'$ are $(0, 0), (2, 2), (1, 0)$. Then, you
    would just graph the triangle and find the equation of the line from $(2,
    2)$ to $(1, 0)$, and setup the bounds accordingly.
  \end{proof}

  \begin{proof}[\textbf{Solution to 2}]
    The Cartesian parametrization of the surface is given by
    \[%
      \r(x, y) = \langle x, y, 2\sqrt{x^2 + y^2} \rangle
    .\]%

    Converting $z = 2\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$ to polar coordinates, we have $z = 2r$.
    Using the polar parametrization, we have
    \[%
      \r(r, \theta) = \langle r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta, 2r \rangle
    .\]%

    Converting $z = 2\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$ to spherical coordinates, we have
    \[%
      z = 2\sqrt{\rho^2\sin^2(\phi)\cos^2(\theta) + \rho^2\sin^2(\phi)\sin^2(\theta)} = 2\rho\sin(\phi)
    .\]%
    Using the spherical parametrization, we have
    \[%
      \r(\rho, \phi, \theta) = \langle \rho\sin(\phi)\cos(\theta), \rho\sin(\phi)\sin(\theta), 2\rho\sin(\phi) \rangle
    .\qedhere\]%
  \end{proof}

  \begin{proof}[\textbf{Solution to 3}]
    For the FTLI, we have to state the following:

    Let $\FF = \langle y^2z + 2xz^2, 2xyz, xy^2 + 2x^2z \rangle$. We need to
    start by first checking if $\FF$ is conservative by taking the curl of
    $\FF$
    \[%
      \curl(\FF) = \nabla \times \FF = \begin{vmatrix}
        \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \\
        \pdv{}/{x} & \pdv{}/{y} & \pdv{}/{z} \\
        y^2z + 2xz^2 & 2xyz & xy^2 + 2x^2z
      \end{vmatrix} = \zero
    .\]%
    Since the curl is zero and $\FF$ is simply connected, we can conclude that
    $\FF$ is conservative. Therefore, we can find a potential function $f(x, y,
    z)$ such that $\nabla f = \FF$.

    Now, we can start with the computation to find $f$.

    I'm going to start by integrating $Q$ with respect to $y$ to get
    \[%
      f = \int 2xyz \dy = xy^2z + g(x, z)
    .\]%
    Differentiating the whole thing with respect to $x$ gives us
    \[%
      f_x = y^2z + g_x(x, z)
    .\]%
    Setting it equal to $P$ gives us
    \[%
      f_x = P \implies y^2z + g_x(x, z) = y^2z + 2xz^2 \implies g_x(x, z) = 2xz^2
    .\]%
    Now, integrating $g_x$ with respect to $x$ gives us
    \[%
      g(x, z) = \int 2xz^2 \dx = x^2z^2 + h(z)
    .\]%
    Now, plugging everything back into $f$, we get
    \[%
      f = xy^2z + x^2z^2 + h(z)
    .\]%
    Next, we differentiate $f$ with respect to $z$ and set it equal to $R$ to
    solve for $h(z)$
    \[%
      f_z = xy^2 + 2x^2z + h'(z)
    .\]%
    Setting it equal to $R$ gives us
    \[%
      f_z = R \implies xy^2 + 2x^2z + h'(z) = xy^2 + 2x^2z \implies h'(z) = 0
    .\]%
    Therefore, $h(z) = C$, where $C$ is a constant, but we can ignore it, as we
    don't want a specific solution, just a solution. Finally, we have
    \[%
      f(x, y, z) = xy^2z + x^2z^2
    .\]%
    Now, using the FTLI, we get
    \[%
      \empheq{\int_C \FF \cdot \d\mathbf{r} = f(\r(b)) - f(\r(a))}
    \qedhere\]%
  \end{proof}

  \begin{proof}[\textbf{Solution to 5}]
    The surface element is given by
    \[%
      \dS = \sqrt{1 + \left(\pdv{z}{x}\right)^2 + \left(\pdv{z}{y}\right)^2} \dA
    .\]%
    Solving for $z$ in terms of $x$ and $y$, we have
    \[%
      z = \frac{1 - x^2 + y - 8}{2}
    .\]%
    Therefore, we have
    \[%
      \dS = \sqrt{1 + 4x^2 + \frac{1}{4}} = \frac{\sqrt{16x^2 + 5}}{2}
    .\]%
    Graphing the triangle and finding the bounds, we have $0 \le x \le 1$ and $0
    \le y \le 4x$. Therefore, the surface area is given by
    \begin{align*}
      \iint_S \dS &= \iint_D \sqrt{1 + 4x^2 + \frac{1}{4}} \dA \\
                  &= \iint_D \frac{\sqrt{16x^2 + 5}}{2} \dA \\
                  &= \int_0^1 \int_0^{4x} \frac{\sqrt{16x^2 + 5}}{2} \dy \dx \\
                  &= \int_0^1 \left[\frac{\sqrt{16x^2 + 5}}{2} y\right]_0^{4x} \dx \\
                  &= \int_0^1 2x\sqrt{16x^2 + 5} \dx
    .\end{align*}
    Using $u$-sub, let $u = 16x^2 + 5$, then $\du = 32x \dx \implies
    \sfrac{\du}{32} = x \dx$. The bounds change from $x = 0 \implies u(0) = 5$
    and $x = 1 \implies u(1) = 21$. Therefore, we have
    \begin{align*}
      \int_0^1 2x\sqrt{16x^2 + 5} \dx &= \int_5^{21} \frac{2\sqrt{u}}{32} \du \\
                                      &= \frac{1}{16} \left[\frac{2}{3} u^{\sfrac{3}{2}}\right]_5^{21} \\
                                      &= \frac{1}{24} \left[21^{\sfrac{3}{2}} - 5^{\sfrac{3}{2}}\right]
    .\qedhere\end{align*}
  \end{proof}

  \begin{proof}[\textbf{Solution to 6}]
    For Stokes' theorem, you have to state the following:

    Given a closed boundary $\pd{S}$ of a surface $S$, which is piecewise smooth
    and positively oriented, we apply Stokes' theorem. Given that the closed
    boundary $\pd{S}$ has an orientation induced on it, we use the right hand
    rule to get the orientation of the surface $S$ to keep the orientation
    consistent.

    Then, you find the curl, the bounds, and the surface element. Remember,
    \[%
      \empheq{\oint_{\pd{S}} \FF \cdot \drr = \iint_S (\nabla \times \FF) \cdot \dSS = \iint_S (\nabla \times \FF) \cdot \frac{\r_x \times \r_y}{\lVert \r_x \times \r_y \rVert} \lVert \r_x \times \r_y \rVert \dA = \iint_S (\nabla \times \FF) \cdot \r_x \times \r_y \dA}
    \qedhere\]%
  \end{proof}

  \begin{proof}[\textbf{Solution to 7}]
    For the Divergence Theorem, you have to state the following:

    Given a closed and piecewise smooth surface $S$ that bounds a solid region
    $E$ in space, we apply the Divergence Theorem. The outward orientation of
    $S$ is induced naturally. Remember,
    \[%
      \empheq{\oiint_S \FF \cdot \dSS = \iiint_E (\nabla \cdot \FF) \dV}
    \qedhere\]%
  \end{proof}
